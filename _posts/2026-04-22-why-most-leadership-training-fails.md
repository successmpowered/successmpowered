Most leadership training produces informed people, not changed ones.

Participants leave knowing more. They can name the frameworks, describe the competencies, articulate what good looks like. And then they go back to their teams and lead exactly as they did before.

This is not a cynical observation. It is simply what the evidence shows, and what anyone who has run leadership programmes for long enough already knows.

## The gap that matters

The hardest problem in leadership development is not knowledge transfer. It is behaviour change.

A leader can know that they interrupt too much, listen too little, give feedback too rarely, and make decisions before the room has had a chance to think. They can know all of this clearly and still do all of it under pressure. Because knowledge does not change patterns. Only sustained practice, honest feedback, and real accountability do.

Most training is designed to fill the knowledge gap. The behaviour gap is harder to close, takes longer, and cannot be achieved in a single day.

## What actually works

Programmes that produce lasting change tend to share four characteristics.

**They run over time.** Not a single day or a single week. Months. Long enough for participants to try things, fail, reflect, and try again with support.

**They combine cohort work with individual coaching.** The group creates accountability and normalises the difficulty of change. The coaching creates the space to work on what is actually happening, not what the curriculum says should be happening.

**They are honest about the self.** Self-awareness is not the soft end of leadership development. It is the foundation. Leaders who do not understand their own defaults cannot choose how they show up. Every other capability gets filtered through those defaults.

**They connect to real work.** Practice assignments are not abstract exercises. They are real conversations the leader needs to have, real decisions they need to make differently, real feedback they need to seek and sit with.

## The question worth asking

Before commissioning a leadership programme, the question worth asking is not "what content will it cover?" but "what will be different in six months, and how will we know?"

If the answer is vague, the programme probably is too.
