# 06 — Design Direction

A premium consultancy site feels different from a template. The difference is usually invisible to the client and obvious to the designer. This document specifies the direction so that whoever builds it produces something that reads as *serious, modern, and human* — not *another nice Webflow template.*

---

## The overall design thesis

**An editorial publication, not a brochure.**

The strongest reference points for Success Empowered's next website are not other leadership training websites. They are:
- A long-form editorial magazine (think *Harvard Business Review*, *Works in Progress*, *The Atlantic*'s redesign).
- A premium consultancy (McKinsey, BCG, Heidrick & Struggles, Spencer Stuart — especially their boutique-style practice pages).
- A design-led B2B firm (IDEO, Doberman, ustwo, Pentagram's client-site work).

The site should feel like something **worth reading**, not just worth scanning. Confidence in typography, whitespace, and the willingness to let a strong sentence occupy a whole screen are the signatures of premium.

## Layout principles

### 1. One idea per section
Each section holds one idea, one mood, one purpose. Don't stuff three concepts into one screen. Long pages with clear sections beat short, dense pages every time for this type of buyer.

### 2. Generous whitespace
Premium is built in the whitespace. Sections should have breathing room vertically (target 120–200px of vertical padding on desktop between major sections). Text blocks should have breathing room horizontally (max measure 65–75 characters for body text).

### 3. Asymmetric editorial rhythm
Alternate full-width editorial sections with tighter grid sections. Avoid the template pattern of alternating coloured bands of equal-size content blocks — it reads as slide deck, not publication.

### 4. Typographic scale
Use a bigger type scale than feels comfortable. Headlines at 56–80px desktop are now standard on premium sites. Large doesn't mean loud — it means confident.

### 5. Hero with restraint
Hero section should be quiet, not busy. One clear headline, one supporting line, one CTA. Don't layer sliders, animations, and stock video over it.

## Typography

### Recommended direction

**Heading typeface:** A modern serif or a confident sans with real character.
- **Serif direction** (recommended): *GT Alpina*, *Söhne Breit*, *Tiempos Headline*, *Canela Deck*, or more accessible options like *Fraunces* (open source), *Source Serif Pro*. Serifs signal editorial authority and maturity. Correctly chosen, they make a consultancy look 10 years older in the right way.
- **Sans direction** (alternative): *GT America*, *Söhne*, *Inter Display*, *Graphik*, *Neue Haas Grotesk*. Cleaner, more modern. Riskier — easier to look like every other B2B site.

**Body typeface:** A highly legible neutral sans.
- *Inter*, *Söhne*, *Graphik*, *Neue Haas Grotesk*, *GT America Mono* for accent only.
- Set at 18–20px on desktop for body copy. Line height 1.5–1.65.

**Recommended pairing (safe, strong):**
- Headings: *Fraunces* (open source) or *GT Alpina* (licensed)
- Body: *Inter* (open source)

**Recommended pairing (bolder):**
- Headings: *Söhne Breit* or *GT Sectra Display*
- Body: *Söhne*

### Typography rules
- **Only two typefaces** across the site. Three at absolute maximum, with the third used only for accent.
- **Use weight, not colour, for emphasis** in body copy. Bold > highlight > italic.
- **Never centre long paragraphs.** Left-align body copy. Centre only for short headlines or pull quotes.
- **Headline sentence case**, not Title Case. Lowercase beginnings of continuing lines. This is a small but unmistakable premium-tier signal.

## Colour palette

The current blue/purple/orange is workable but generic. Refine rather than replace.

### Recommended refined palette

```
Primary Navy        #0E1E3F   — headings, anchors, wordmark
Deep Indigo         #2A2560   — secondary accent, hero backgrounds
Warm Clay (accent)  #C96A3F   — CTAs, key highlights (evolution of current orange)
Ivory               #F6F1E8   — warm off-white section background
Pure White          #FFFFFF   — primary background
Slate Text          #1B1B1E   — body text (not black — feels softer, more considered)
Muted Gray          #6B6E76   — secondary text, captions
Line Gray           #E2E0DA   — dividers, card borders
```

Why these choices:
- **Navy + ivory + clay** is the palette of editorial magazines, premium book jackets, and serious professional services firms. It reads as adult and durable.
- The **clay/rust** tone (replacing the current orange) warms the palette and differentiates from the sea of "corporate blue + pure white" sites. It also references the cross-cultural reach (India) without being literal about it.
- **Not using pure black** for text is a small but meaningful upgrade.

Keep the current blue/purple *geometric motif* concept if you love it, but render it in the new palette — so it reads as refined, not template.

### Colour use rules
- One colour dominates each section. Don't stack coloured blocks.
- The clay accent appears sparingly — on CTAs, one or two highlights per page, and nowhere else. Scarcity makes it feel premium.
- Dark-mode version of the site is a nice-to-have, not a must.

## Imagery

This is the biggest single visual upgrade you can make.

### Commission, don't stock

Budget for a proper photography session. The difference between commissioned and stock on this tier of site is stark and immediate.

### What to photograph

- **Christopher and Aditi**, in their working environments, in considered portrait style. Not corporate headshots against a grey wall. Think New York Times Magazine profile portraits — composed, quiet, slightly atmospheric.
- **Workplace scenes** that reflect the buyer's world — modern offices, senior-level rooms, small-group workshops, quiet one-to-one conversations. Shot with natural light, not flash. Real organisational context.
- **Workshop / programme delivery** — if clients will allow — genuine moments from the firm's actual work. Nothing else will read as real.

### What to avoid

- Diverse-happy-team-around-a-laptop stock photography.
- "Business people shaking hands" stock.
- Generic lifestyle images unconnected to the firm's actual work.
- Heavy filter/colour-grading that makes everyone look the same synthetic orange.

### Treatment

- Use a **consistent colour grade** across all commissioned photography so the site has one visual voice.
- Consider **duotone treatments** (navy + ivory, or navy + clay) for one class of imagery — for example in hero banners or case study headers — which ties photography to brand palette without being gimmicky.

## Iconography & graphic language

### Replace generic line icons

The current icon set reads as stock. A premium site either:
- Has **custom iconography** designed as a system (expensive but high-leverage), or
- **Has no iconography at all**, letting typography and imagery carry the visual weight.

For Success Empowered at this stage, I'd recommend **minimal custom iconography** — 8 to 12 custom symbols used sparingly across service pillars, approach steps, and outcomes. Keep them geometric, confident, single-weight.

### The circular motif

Keep the circular/geometric element already in the brand — but refine it. Make it a repeatable brand device (e.g. a single circle that appears near major section breaks, or a subtle circular mark on case study cards). One consistent device beats a scattered collection of shapes.

---

## Animation & interaction

- **Subtle, editorial motion** — fades on scroll, gentle reveals, smooth anchor scrolls. Nothing bouncy, nothing playful, nothing video-game.
- **Hover states matter.** Links, buttons, cards should have considered hover treatments that feel designed, not default.
- **No sliders on the homepage.** Sliders hide content; premium sites show content.
- **No intro animations.** Users are impatient and sophisticated.
- **Scroll-linked animation** (parallax, sticky sections) used sparingly and only where it earns its weight.

## Specific section-level design notes

### Homepage hero
- Full-viewport height acceptable; sub-viewport preferred on desktop if content below the fold needs to be visible.
- Headline left-aligned, large, with generous line height.
- Supporting line below, smaller, muted gray.
- Single primary CTA, styled as a solid button in clay/rust.
- One quiet secondary text link underneath.
- Background: either ivory or a single editorial image with a subtle darkening overlay for text legibility.

### Service cards
- Not boxed with strong borders. Use subtle line rules or simply whitespace + typography.
- Each card has: a small label ("Leadership Development"), a one-sentence description, and a linked action ("Explore →").
- Hover: small animation, colour shift, or subtle motion.

### Case study cards
- These should feel like *magazine features*, not generic content cards.
- Each card: image (commissioned or duotone-treated), short label (e.g. *Leadership · Financial Services · 9 months*), strong headline, pull-quote, a named outcome number if possible.

### Team cards
- Large, considered portraits — don't crop faces into small circles.
- Name in the display typeface.
- Short, honest one-line descriptor.
- Link to individual bio page for those who have one.

### Article cards (Insights)
- Editorial pattern. Large image or typographic treatment, topic pill, headline, author byline, read time.
- Grid of three across on desktop.

## Accessibility

- WCAG 2.2 AA minimum. This matters commercially — your buyers' organisations will evaluate.
- Colour contrast ratios checked on every combination.
- Full keyboard navigation.
- Proper semantic HTML.
- Alt text on every image.
- Accessible forms (labels, error messages, focus states).

---

## What the result should feel like

Close the laptop. Reopen it a day later. The site should feel:
- **Adult.** Like a firm that has been doing serious work for years.
- **Warm.** Not corporate-cold. Actual humans run this.
- **Sharp.** The thinking is current; the language is precise.
- **Quiet confidence.** Nothing is shouting. Everything is carrying weight.

That is the signature. Every design decision should be tested against it.
